Co-pilot in Comair crash noted lack of runway lights was 'weird'

THE ASSOCIATED PRESS
  • Transcript of and report on cockpit voice recording
  • Transcript of air traffic control communications
  • More Lexington crash coverage from our sister site, Kentucky.com

WASHINGTON - WASHINGTON - Seconds before the crash of a commuter plane that was headed down the wrong runway, the co-pilot noted it was "weird" that the Lexington, Ky., strip had no lights, according to a flight-recorder transcript released Wednesday.

The runway where Comair Flight 5191 attempted to take off in the pre-dawn darkness Aug. 27 wasn't long enough for a passenger jet.

Co-pilot James Polehinke said it was "weird with no lights," just seconds before the sound of the impact was heard, according to the National Transportation Safety Board transcript. The captain, Jeffrey Clay, then responded, "Yeah."

The crash killed 49 of 50 people on board. Polehinke, the lone survivor, lost a leg and suffered brain damage from the crash. He has told family members he remembers nothing about that morning.

One of those killed was Pat Smith, a Habitat for Humanity volunteer from Lexington on his way to South Mississippi to help in reconstruction after Hurricane Katrina.

Sixteen of the passengers suffered from smoke inhalation, indicating they survived the initial impact, according to an injury report released Wednesday by the NTSB. Other passengers sustained a variety of internal and brain injuries, broken bones, amputated limbs and burns, according to the report.

At 6:26 p.m. on Aug. 26 - just under 12 hours before the crash - Polehinke ordered chicken fingers and two beers from the hotel bar, according to a restaurant receipt released by NTSB. The server told investigators Polehinke didn't open the beers while at the bar but took them with him.

According to Comair procedures, also listed in the report, pilots can be fired if they consume alcohol within 12 hours of the scheduled departure of a flight.

Comair spokeswoman Kate Marx said Polehinke is still considered a first officer for Comair. FAA regulations prohibit pilots from consuming alcohol eight hours before a flight.

Marx said alcohol was not a factor in the pilots' performance the next day.

"Both pilots tested negative," Marx said.

According to federal investigators, Clay taxied the plane onto the wrong runway before Polehinke took over the controls for takeoff from Lexington's Blue Grass Airport.

Polehinke said, "I'll take us to Atlanta," and Clay responds, "sure."

According to the transcript, the last understandable word - "whoa" - came from Clay just a second before the crash.

It was the first time the public was given access to the transcripts of what the Comair pilots told each other in the cockpit during the ill-fated flight.

In a brief statement, Comair said, "We recognize the investigation is a long and difficult process for the families, especially when announcements - such as today's - receive intense public scrutiny. Our desire is to learn as much as we can in order to prevent these kinds of accidents from happening again."

Erlanger, Ky.-based Comair, along with its parent, Atlanta-based Delta Air Lines Inc., are in bankruptcy proceedings.

Numerous lawsuits have been filed accusing Comair of negligence. However, the airline has sued Blue Grass Airport and the Federal Aviation Administration, contending they are partially responsible.

A week before the crash, the taxiways at Blue Grass were altered as part of a construction project, but the maps and charts used in the cockpits of Comair and other airlines were not updated. The FAA did notify airlines of the changes through a separate announcement.

Only one controller was on duty when the plane crashed. The veteran controller cleared the flight for takeoff, then turned away to do administrative work, officials said. He didn't see the plane turn down the wrong runway.

Among the documents released Wednesday by the NTSB is a letter Blue Grass Airport tower manager Duff Ortman sent the FAA on Jan. 12, 2006. It requests two additional controllers at the airport or a larger overtime budget to comply with agency guildelines requiring two people work the overnight or "mid" shift at the tower.

"There is no more rubber band to stretch," Ortman said.

Despite the directive, only one controller was working the midshift at the time of the crash. In e-mails obtained by The Associated Press three weeks after the crash, high-ranking FAA officials called Ortman a "renegade" and suggested he would be fired.

The Comair jet was supposed to take off from the 7,000-foot main runway, runway 22, but instead used the 3,500-foot runway 26, which is meant only for smaller planes.

As they prepared for takeoff, Polehinke asked, "what runway," and inquired about runway 24 - which does not exist. Clay immediately responded, "It's 22."

An engineering report by NTSB concluded the flight crew "unknowingly" took off from the shorter runway.

Leading up to takeoff, the pilot and co-pilot discussed their families, co-workers and job opportunities. Clay and Polehinke also discussed flying around some potentially rough weather. Clay also addressed the passengers, telling them to expect a flight of a little more than an hour.

Louise Roselle, one of the attorneys representing victims' families, said the conversation between Clay and Polehinke about searching for other jobs reinforces one of the central issues in the lawsuits related to the crash.

"It reinforces how Comair has been treating its pilots," Roselle said.

Also, some of the NTSB's findings will help clarify the case against Bombardier, the Canadian company that built the airplane, Roselle said. Roselle said there is a question of whether the plane was properly constructed.

"This plane did not get more than about 8 feet off the ground. All but one person died. You have to ask the question of why did they die," Roselle said.
