Fox subpoenas YouTube over '24'


Reuters

Twentieth Century Fox Film served YouTube with a subpoena on Wednesday demanding that the Google-owned viral-video site disclose the identity of a user who uploaded copies of entire recent episodes of prime-time series 24 and The Simpsons.

The subpoena, which first came to light on the blog Google Watch, was granted by a judge in US District Court for the Northern District of California after being filed by the News Corp-owned studio on 18 January. It is not yet known whether YouTube has complied with the request.

In addition, a second, lesser-known video site, LiveDigital, was also served with a similar subpoena.

A Fox representative confirmed that the subpoena was filed and served to both YouTube and LiveDigital but declined further comment. A representative of YouTube declined comment.

The 24 episodes in question actually appeared on YouTube prior to their prime-time 14 January premiere on the Fox broadcast network, which spread four hour-long episodes of the hit drama over two consecutive nights. Fox became aware that the episodes were on YouTube on 8 January, according to the subpoena.

Filed on the basis of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act, the subpoena includes testimony of Fox Entertainment Group vice president Jane Sunderland, suggesting that Fox has been unable to determine the video uploaders' identities on its own. The uploaded material could cause Fox "irreparable harm," Sunderland said, but it was not immediately clear if the episodes in question still were posted on the site or perhaps had been removed.

However, the subpoena identifies the YouTube subscriber by the username 'ECOtotal'. A search under that username on the YouTube site unearths a user by that name with a banner across the top of the subscriber's page that reads, "This user account has been suspended." Still, identifying ECOtotal won't necessarily explain how unaired episodes of 24 made it onto the Internet. Prior to 8 January, there were reports that the same episodes had popped up on illegal file-sharing sites, which may have transmitted them even before they appeared on YouTube.

This is not an unprecedented request for YouTube. Last May, prior to its $1.65bn acquisition by Google, the site complied with a request made by Paramount Pictures to identify a user who posted a clip from the film Twin Towers.

But Google has a history of fighting subpoenas seeking the names of those using its services.

YouTube and most other similar sites typically tell content providers that they will delete copyright video when alerted by owners of the material.

Among the content companies, much of the more aggressive policing of peer-to-peer and community-based Web sites has been by Universal Music Group. The world's biggest record company has sued MySpace.com -- which, like Fox, is owned by Rupert Murdoch's News Corp -- and others over what it calls illegal postings of its artists' music videos, and it came close to legal action against YouTube before striking a licensing agreement with YouTube last year.

Terence Clark, a copyright attorney at the Los Angeles law firm Greenberg Traurig, said Fox appears to be walking along a fine legal line.

"It's the process available under the Digital Millennium Copyright Act," Clark said. "There are certain procedures you can follow to get some information, but this also impinges on the question of the privacy issues of the users of the sites."

Some sites might need to defend strongly against such actions as Fox is taking, but ultimately, the studio is likely to prevail, said Tom Ferber, a copyright attorney at the Pryor Cashman Sherman & Flynn law firm in New York.

"It's always a policy decision of the entity involved," Ferber noted. "So if you're the hard-news press, for instance, usually money is no object if it's seen as infringing on your rights. And these sites may have business issues of concern as well. But I think ultimately, the studio is going to get the names that they want."
