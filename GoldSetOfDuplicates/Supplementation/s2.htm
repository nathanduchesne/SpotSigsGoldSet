Folic acid 'may boost memory in old age'

19/01/2007

Folic acid supplementation may help to improve cognitive functions such as memory in old age, new research claims.

Cognitive function declines with age, in particular in the areas of memory and how fast things are processed, and has been linked with dementia.

Researchers from Wageningen University and Wageningen Centre for Food Sciences, Netherlands, gave 818 older people daily folic acid supplements or a placebo for three years.

Publishing their findings in the Lancet medical journal today, the researchers saw a marked improvement in cognitive functions in adults given folic acid instead of the placebo.

Previous research has suggested that low folate and raised homocysteine concentrations in the blood are important risk factors for poor cognitive performance.

Commenting on this latest study, the researchers wrote: "We have shown that three-year folic acid supplementation improves performance on tests that measure information processing speed and memory, domains that are known to decline with age.

"[Our study was conducted] in older adults with raised total homocysteine concentrations."

They add that similar studies should now be carried out in other populations "to provide greater insight into the clinical relevance of folic acid supplementation, such as in populations with mild cognitive impairment and dementia".
