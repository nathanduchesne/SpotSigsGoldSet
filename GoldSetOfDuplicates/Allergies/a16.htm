Winter no relief for allergy sufferers

REBECCA SANTANA
Associated Press

TRENTON, N.J. - If you're reaching for the cold medicine and chicken soup to battle an onset of wheezing and sniffling this winter, hold on. Doctors say the unseasonably warm weather has flooded some offices with patients suffering from an unusual ailment this time of year: allergies.

"The phone is ringing off the hook - it's incredible," said Dr. Clifford W. Bassett, vice chairman in charge of public education for the American Academy of Allergy, Asthma and Immunology.

Doctors say this winter's weather has sparked an onslaught of mold spores that cause allergies and fluctuating temperatures that irritate already-suffering nasal passages. Many patients may confuse an allergic reaction with a common cold.

"It's an explosion of people who are realizing that they may have allergies," said Bassett, who has a practice in Manhattan. "Typically, January and February are quiet times in most allergists' offices."

Maria Carola, 35, has been taking shots for years to deal with her chronic allergies, but can still go for long periods without her medication and do just fine - especially in the winter when the pollen count is almost nonexistent.

Not this winter. She went to the doctor Friday complaining of typical allergy-related symptoms such as a runny nose and itchy eyes. She said she almost felt as if she were coming down with a cold, and was feeling awful for this time of year.

"Usually, the symptoms wouldn't be that prominent" even without her medication, said Carola, who lives in New York.

Mold spores that grow outdoors would normally die off during a cold snap or be covered with snow. But in warm weather mold spores continue to grow and spread. Throwing open the windows to enjoy the weather makes things worse: The mold spores waltz inside.

"For anyone who is allergic to mold, they would have stopped their medications when the weather gets cold," Dr. Maria Lania-Howarth, who heads the Allergy and Immunology Department at Cooper University Hospital in Camden. "They're going to be a lot more miserable."

Changing temperatures can also aggravate allergy problems. In Maple Grove, Minn., a suburb of Minneapolis, temperatures have hovered in the 40s, said Dr. Pramod Kelkar, who has seen a slight increase in allergy patients during what is normally a quiet time.

He said mold is to blame for some of the problems, but fluctuating temperatures can also irritate people whose noses and sinuses are already inflamed.

The most common allergy symptoms are itchy eyes, noses and throats, and possibly runny or stuffy noses, said Bassett. People with colds may share the stuffed up nose problem, but also may see changes in the color of their mucus, loss of appetite, fatigue, low-grade headaches or fevers.

Experts have differing opinions on whether a continued warm spell would mean an early onset of tree pollen - usually considered the beginning of allergy season. In New Jersey, that usually takes place in late February or early March.

Some doctors say a warm spell could bring a very early allergy season since trees might begin to bud early; others say the temperatures don't always make much of a difference.

"We can have a very cold or a very warm winter, and it will have no effect on when these things come out," said Dr. John Weiler, president of CompleWare Corp., a company that does clinical trials on allergy medications for the pharmaceutical industry.
