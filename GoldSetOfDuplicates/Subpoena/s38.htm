By Nicole Urbanowicz
Of DOW JONES NEWSWIRES

NEW YORK -(Dow Jones)- News Corp.'s (NWS) Twentieth Century Fox has subpoenaed Google Inc.'s (GOOG) YouTube video-sharing site to unveil the identity of a user who uploaded pirated copies of recent episodes of "24" and "The Simpsons."

The subpoena filed Jan. 18 in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California requests that YouTube disclose information sufficient to identify the subscriber so that Fox can stop the infringing immediately.

According to the subpoena's declaration made by Jane Sunderland, vice president of content protection for Twentieth Century Fox, the four-episode season premiere of "24" episode appeared on the site ahead of its TV broadcast on YouTube and 12 episodes of the Simpsons were being distributed on the site by a subscriber identified as "ECOtotal."

On Jan. 8, Fox said it officially notified YouTube about the infringed episodes and requested immediate removal or to disable access to the service. News about the subpoena filed surfaced on the blog, Google Watch.

The same group of episodes were uploaded under the username of Jorge Romero on the video site LiveDigital, to which Fox also issued a subpoena.

A spokesman for LiveDigital said the material was taken down right away and LiveDigital intends to comply with the subpoena immediately.

A recent YouTube search revealed only a prequel to Season 6 of "24" and several clips from The Simpsons episodes.

This isn't the first time the Twentieth Century Fox has had a piracy issue involving YouTube, although a young user was identified in a case in which a Family Guy episode was posted on YouTube ahead of its premiere. However, in this recent case, Fox noted in the subpoena it has been unable to discover through its own investigation the identity of the subscriber.

The issue of piracy has sparked concern among entertainment companies and music and video-sharing sites, along with legal implications of whether the sharing sites should be financially accountable.

Excluding damages related to piracy of TV episodes, the Motion Picture Association estimates that major global movie studios lost about $2.3 billion to Internet piracy in 2005.

It is unknown whether YouTube has responded to the subpoena at this point and calls to YouTube weren't immediately returned.
