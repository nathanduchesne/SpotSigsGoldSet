Final moments of Comair Flight 5191
THE ASSOCIATED PRESS AND THE ENQUIRER

WASHINGTON – Seconds before the nation’s deadliest plane crash in nearly 5 years, the co-pilot aboard Comair Flight 5191 noted it was “weird” that the runway at Lexington, had no lights, according to a transcript released today.

The Aug. 27 crash of the regional airliner killed 49 of 50 people on board. The flight crashed in the pre-dawn darkness shortly after takeoff from the wrong runway, which was unlit and too short for a passenger jet.

The transcript was one of nearly 50 documents released this morning by the National Transportation Safety Board, which is studying the crash.


The safety board is expected to issue a final report - including a ruling on what caused it - in late spring or early summer.

Comair's flight crew procedures and training are a focus of questioning in some of the documents. NTSB officials interviewed other captains and safety officials at the Erlanger-based carrier in the days after the crash.

Three specific areas singled out in the questions include:

* Whether Comair requires crews to double check that they have the right runway or heading before takeoff. Such a check is not part of the standard Comair pre-flight check list.

* Whether the company's rules prohibit taking off from an unlit runway at night. The fact that the Lexington runway was unlit could have been a cue that it wasn't suitable for use by a commercial jet.

* Whether Comair rules prohibit using a 75-foot wide runway - another cue that the runway in question might have been unsuitable for a safe takeoff.

The investigators also questioned other Comair pilots and first officers about the flight crew, and learned they were both "by the book" professionals.

According to the NTSB transcript, first officier James Polehinke – the lone crash survivor – said it was “weird with no lights,” just seconds before the crash impact was heard. The flight’s captain, Jeffrey Clay, responded, “Yeah.”

Polehinke lost a leg and suffered brain damage from the crash. He has told family members he remembers nothing about that morning.

According to the transcript, the last understandable word that wasn't swearing came from Clay just a second before the crash, when he said, “Whoa.”

Minutes earlier, Polehinke said, “I’ll take us to Atlanta,” and Clay responded, "sure.”

According to federal investigators, Clay taxied the plane onto the wrong runway before Polehinke took over the controls for takeoff from Lexington’s Blue Grass Airport.

As they were preparing for takeoff, Polehinke asked, “what runway,” and inquired about runway 24 – which doesn't exist at the Lexington airport. Clay immediately responded, “It’s 22” – which was the runway they were supposed to be on.

The regional jet took off from a 3,500-foot runway - unlit because it is not for use by commercial airliners - instead of the 7,000-foot main runway where the jet was supposed to be.

Less than a minute before the crash, Clay said, “All yours Jim.” Polehinke responds, “My brakes, my controls.”

In a brief statement, Comair said, “We recognize the investigation is a long and difficult process for the families, especially when announcements – such as today’s – receive intense public scrutiny. Our desire is to learn as much as we can in order to prevent these kinds of accidents from happening again.”

Only one controller was on duty when the plane crashed.

In a supplement to crash report, the controller on duty, Christopher Damron, said he did not actually witness the crash itself. He said he turned his back to do paperwork then heard it.

“I turned around to do the traffic count, heard a crash and saw a fireball west of the airport,” wrote Damron, who had worked at the tower since 1988.

In another interview with investigators, a ramp agent with Comair said the flight crew boarded the wrong plane at first. He said first officer Polehinke appeared “disappointed or surprised” they boarded the wrong craft. But the agency recalled no discussion from the crew about running late.

“The first officer came down from the aircraft and was not really disgusted but did not know how he got on the wrong plane, the captain got off and said it was going to be one of thse days but oh well what are you going to do," the agent said.

Another ramp agent said the pilot (Polehinke) that did the walk around inspection of the aircraft appeared “worried.”

When asked if the crew ever seemed rushed, he replied not rushed or hurried but maybe "worried." He said when the pilot did the walk around of the correct plane he did it with a "little bit of worry" and was just walking fast and looked just to the ground. He was not chatting with the ramp agents.”

Since the crash, federal agencies have suggested safety improvements.

Last week, the FAA suggested U.S. airports beef up information regarding ongoing runway and taxiway construction and closures given to airlines. The agency conceded the current notification system to pilots, airlines and base operators “may be inadequate.” The agency suggested a method of distributing up-to-date maps of ongoing construction in addition to written advisories.

Last month, the NTSB suggested to the FAA to enact two new safety requirements. It suggested: Commercial aircraft crews must be given rules to confirm and cross-check their aircraft’s location at the assigned runway before getting into position for takeoff; and commercial carriers must establish specific guidelines for pilots on runway lighting requirements for nighttime takeoffs.
