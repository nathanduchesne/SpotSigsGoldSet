What do you think is the top story of the year?
CHURCH ARSON. A student from Word of Life Bible Institute is arrested on charges he set the May fire that destroyed the Pottersville Episcopal Church.
ETHAN ALLEN AFTERMATH. The NTSB issues a report blaming the boat's instability on its canopy and its passenger load.
NO HOCKEY. Barry Melrose folds the Frostbite after failing to find a new owner. The Glens Falls Civic Center has it first no-hockey season since the 1970s.
TECH JOBS ANNOUNCEMENT. Plans are announced in June for a multibillion AMD semiconductor plant in Luther Forest Technology Park
SWEENEY'S DEFEAT. Republican Congressman John Sweeney is defeated in November by newcomer Democrat Kirsten Gillibrand.
INDOOR WATER PARK. Great Escape opens a hotel with an indoor water park across from the Queensbury amusement park.
THOUSANDS IN COLD. Thousands in the region go without power for up to three days during a bitter late winter cold spell when high winds topple power lines.
TOWNHOUSES PLAN. The Glens Falls Common Council, after pressure from residents, blocks a plan to demolish the existing subsidized complex and rebuild at two locations.
DOWNTOWN HOTEL. Glens Falls officials announce plans for a hotel/parking/office complex that would connect to the Civic Center. No specific tenants are announced.
MURDER TRIAL, SENTENCING. Shawn Doyle is convicted and sentenced in murder of a girlfriend, whose body was found in a submerged toolbox in Washington County.
WEST MOUNTAIN HOMES. Mike Brandt, owner of West Mountain Ski Area, announces plans for 2,400 hilltop homes on the opposite side of the mountain in Lake Luzerne.
TOO HOT TO RACE. Temperatures soar into the upper 90s with heat indexes as high as 110, and forcing NYRA to cancel a Saratoga Race Course nine-race card in early August, believed the first time ever.
MURDER IN GRANVILLE. A massive police search unfolds for a Granville man, Kelly J. Roberts, who police believe beat his wife to death with a baseball bat. Roberts' body is found a day later in a densely wooded area of western Vermont.
SARATOGA SPRINGS REFERENDUM. Voters decide to retain the commissioner form of government in the city. A switch to a mayor-council system had been proposed.
EMINENT DOMAIN ACTIONS. The village of Corinth begins an eminent domain process to take the former International Paper Co. mill property from its New York City owners, and the Fort Edward Town Board moves to acquire the land for PCB dewatering plant.
GORE MOUNTAIN PLANS. Gov. George Pataki announces plans for a $7 million expansion of Gore Mountain Ski Center and an additional $3 million to complete the railroad line connection between Saratoga and North Creek.
KIDNAPPING IN HAITI. Charles Adams, the former local broadcaster and team owner, is held overnight in July by kidnappers in Haiti demanding $70,000 ransom. Adams is released after convincing his captors to free him without ransom.
TOWNHOUSE KILLING. A jury finds a Cohoes man guilty of a fatal stabbing at Henry Hudson Townhouses in Glens Falls. William A. Henderson is sentenced to 30 years to life in state prison. His accomplices get lesser sentences after turning state's evidence.
ATTEMPTED ABDUCTION TRIAL, SENTENCING. John Regan, of Connecticut, is sentenced to 12 years in prison for attempting to kidnap a 17-year-old student Lindsey Ferguson in 2005. He had pleaded guilty in May to attempted kidnapping.

Iraqi judge says Saddam will be executed by Saturday at the latest

 
Friday, December 29, 2006 12:04 PM EST

BAGHDAD, Iraq (AP) -- Saddam Hussein has been transferred from U.S. custody, his lawyers said, and an Iraqi judge authorized to attend the former dictator's hanging said he would be executed no later than Saturday.

The physical hand-over of Saddam to Iraqi authorities was believed to be one of the last steps before he was to be hanged, although the lawyers' statement did not specifically say Saddam was in Iraqi hands.

"A few minutes ago we received correspondence from the Americans saying that President Saddam Hussein is no longer under the control of U.S. forces," according to the statement faxed to The Associated Press.

"Saddam will be executed today or tomorrow," said Munir Haddad, a judge on the appeals court that upheld Saddam's death sentence. "All the measures have been done."

Haddad is authorized to attend the execution on behalf of the judiciary.

"I am ready to attend and there is no reason for delays," Haddad said.
